Reviewer Mindset Framework

How to think well in domains you do not yet know deeply

Core shift: your job is not to already know the domain. Your job is to find where trust, assumptions, attacker capability, and evidence concentrate, then reduce uncertainty without bluffing.

1. Start from invariants

Before platform details, ask what must never happen.

  • Unauthorized access
  • Secret leakage
  • Integrity loss
  • Unsafe privilege gain
  • Unbounded abuse

2. Translate design into trust bets

Every system quietly says: “we trust this enough.” Surface those bets.

  • We trust this client enough
  • We trust this storage enough
  • We trust this identity claim enough
  • We trust this control to fail safely

3. Think in attacker capabilities

Do not anchor on named bugs. Ask what the attacker can actually do.

  • What can they send?
  • What can they see?
  • What can they steal?
  • What can they replay?
  • What can they persist?

4. Separate claims from evidence

Reassuring language is not verification.

  • Against whom is it secure?
  • By what mechanism?
  • Under which assumptions?
  • How does it fail?
  • How do we verify it?

The Portable Review Loop

Asset What matters here? Who would want it? What is the business harm?
Boundary Where does the system stop assuming and start validating?
Assumption What must be true for this design to be safe?
Capability What can an attacker send, observe, steal, replay, modify, or persist?
Control What control prevents or limits that failure?
Evidence Where is it enforced? How is it tested? What is the runtime proof?
Unknown What do I still not know that materially changes risk?
Learn Which standard, guide, or expert source closes that gap fastest?
Compact mental model:
Unknown domain → find assets → find trust boundaries → write assumptions → test attacker capabilities → map controls → ask for evidence → isolate unknowns → learn only what changes risk.

The Five Question Families

Asset questions

  • What is valuable here?
  • Who would want it?
  • What is the actual business impact?

Boundary questions

  • Where does control change hands?
  • Where does external influence enter?
  • Where does privilege or identity cross a line?

Assumption questions

  • What are we assuming about this component or platform?
  • What if that assumption is only mostly true?
  • Which wrong assumption causes the worst failure?

Capability questions

  • What can the attacker control or observe?
  • What is realistic on this platform?
  • What can they replay or persist?

Evidence questions

  • Show me where this is enforced.
  • Show me how it is tested.
  • Show me what failure looks like.
Failure mode to avoid: trying to sound knowledgeable too early. Better posture: “I do not know this platform deeply yet, so I am going to reason from assets, trust, assumptions, attacker capability, and evidence until the platform-specific gaps become clear.”

How to cross domains

Do not import web controls directly into mobile, AI, cloud, or identity systems. Import question families, then use the domain’s own standard.

  • Web: OWASP ASVS
  • Mobile: OWASP MASVS and MASTG
  • Identity/OAuth: protocol guidance plus system-specific validation rules
  • AI systems: attacker goals, prompt/tool/data boundaries, output trust

Practice habit

After every review, write down:

  1. one assumption you accepted too easily,
  2. one trust boundary you under-modeled,
  3. one control you did not verify deeply enough,
  4. one unknown you should have named earlier.
