Threat Modelling Glossary

Core terms and concepts for threat identification and modelling.

Threat Modelling Fundamentals
Threat Model
A systematic analysis of what could go wrong in a system, who could exploit it, how they might do it, and what the impact would be.
Threat
A potential attack, exploit, or negative event that could harm a system. Example: "An attacker intercepts network traffic."
Vulnerability
A weakness in a system that makes a threat possible. Example: "Unencrypted communication channel."
Attack Vector
The path or method an attacker uses to exploit a vulnerability. Example: "Packet sniffing on an unsecured Wi-Fi network."
Asset
Anything of value in the system that needs protection. Examples: data, users, reputation, availability, confidentiality.
Actor / Threat Actor
A person or entity capable of executing a threat. Can range from script kiddies to nation-state adversaries.
Threat Modelling Frameworks
STRIDE
A taxonomy of threat types: Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of Service, Elevation of Privilege.
Spoofing identity → Attacker impersonates a trusted user
Data Flow Diagram (DFD)
A visual representation showing how data moves through a system, including processes, storage, and external entities.
Mindset & Judgment
Threat Identification Mindset
The trained ability to systematically think like an attacker: asking "where could this break?", "who would attack this?", "what would I target first?" Rather than relying on intuition alone, this is a disciplined, repeatable pattern of questioning.
Attacker's Perspective
Deliberately reversing your normal "how do I build this securely?" question to "how would I break this?" This mental model is essential for uncovering non-obvious threats.
Trust Boundary
A line in a diagram separating areas with different security levels. Threats often occur at trust boundaries where data crosses from trusted to untrusted zones.
AI-Assisted Threat Modelling
AI as Threat Partner
Using an AI (LLM) as a brainstorming and reasoning partner to surface threats you might miss alone. AI excels at recall and combination; human judgment validates and contextualizes.
Human Judgment
The irreducible element of threat modelling: contextual reasoning, understanding business intent, recognizing what matters most, catching AI hallucinations and irrelevant suggestions.
Prompt Engineering (for threats)
Structuring questions to an AI to elicit useful threat identification. Example: "Given this system boundary [description], what attack vectors might target the authentication layer?"
