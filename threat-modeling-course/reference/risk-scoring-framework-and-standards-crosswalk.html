Risk Scoring Framework and Standards Crosswalk

This page gives a quantifiable scoring table for design-phase prioritization and maps it to external references: OWASP Risk Rating Methodology and CVSS.

External references:
  • OWASP Risk Rating Methodology (RRM)

1) Quantifiable Point Model for Design-Phase Threat Risks

Priority Score = Likelihood (1-5) x Impact (1-5) x Confidence Modifier (1.0 / 1.2 / 1.4)

Likelihood (1-5)Definition
1Improbable; high attacker prerequisites and strong controls.
2Low; exploitable only with meaningful constraints.
3Feasible; realistic attacker path with moderate effort.
4Likely; known patterns and weak/missing controls.
5Highly likely; straightforward and repeatable exploitation.
Impact (1-5)Definition
1Minor localized impact; no material business consequence.
2Limited operational/customer effect; quickly recoverable.
3Material service/team impact; possible contractual concerns.
4High multi-team/customer impact; likely legal/compliance concern.
5Critical business impact; major breach, outage, or regulatory consequence.
Confidence ModifierWhen to use
1.0Strong evidence and clear assumptions.
1.2Partial evidence or meaningful unknowns.
1.4Major uncertainty; missing critical evidence.
Score BandPriorityDecision
20+P0Block release/exposure until mitigated and verified.
12-19.9P1Fix this cycle with owner and evidence plan.
6-11.9P2Planned remediation with timeline.
<6P3/P4Monitor, harden, or accept with rationale.
Escalation override: any severity (including Defense in Depth) can escalate when a business-critical asset is materially exposed.

3) Implementation-Ready RRM to 1-5 Mapping

Use OWASP RRM factors to enrich your likelihood and impact estimation, then normalize to this course's deterministic 1-5 model.

RRM Input GroupRRM FactorsNormalization Target
Likelihood inputs Threat agent factors + vulnerability factors (for example skill, motive, opportunity, ease of exploit) Likelihood (1-5)
Impact inputs Technical impact + business impact factors Impact (1-5), with business impact dominant when available
RRM Average BandMapped 1-5 ScoreInterpretation
0 to <31-2Low band; choose 1 if strongly constrained, 2 if feasible under constraints.
3 to <63Medium band; practical and plausible.
6 to 94-5High band; choose 5 when repeatable and broadly exposed, otherwise 4.
Transformation algorithm:
  1. Score selected RRM likelihood factors and compute average.
  2. Map average to Likelihood 1-5 using the band table above.
  3. Score RRM technical and business impact factors and compute averages.
  4. If business impact data exists, use business-impact mapping as primary Impact score; otherwise use technical impact mapping.
  5. Apply confidence modifier (1.0, 1.2, 1.4) based on evidence quality and unknowns.
  6. Compute final priority score and apply local priority bands and escalation overrides.
Guardrail against false precision: Do not expose more than one decimal place in intermediate averages and always include a short rationale narrative. The model is for disciplined consistency, not pseudo-certainty.

4) Challenge: Is OWASP RRM alone enough for this purpose?

Short answer: not by itself.
  • RRM provides a structure, but your organization still needs calibrated scoring anchors and priority thresholds.
  • RRM can produce repeatable ratings only if your factor definitions are tuned to your environment.
  • Design-phase uncertainty requires explicit confidence handling, which many teams omit.

Conclusion: It is possible and appropriate to use OWASP RRM principles for design-phase risk prioritization, but only as a base framework. You still need a deterministic local scoring table, override rules, and governance gates (which this course defines).

5) Implementation Rule of Thumb

  1. Use this matrix for design threats and architecture risks.
  2. Use CVSS for concrete vulnerability findings.
  3. When both exist, prioritize using business-context score and document CVSS as supporting evidence.
